Program at a Glance

EthioWare Research Scholars is built around seven core commitments — the structural elements that make a 16-week research mentorship genuinely work for Ethiopian high schoolers.

16 Weeks — Extended Foundations Phase

The program devotes its first four weeks entirely to building research literacy before any paper writing begins.

Mentor Pods: 1 Mentor per 3–4 Students

Small, consistent pods replace pure 1-on-1 mentorship, building peer accountability alongside expert guidance.

English Academic Writing Support — Built In

Dedicated writing coach sessions address academic English fluency directly, without stigma, as a core program pillar.

Learning Materials, Always Within Reach

PDF templates and writing tutorials live on the LMS, with a Telegram support group for real-time help and reminders.

Ethiopian Diaspora Mentor Pipeline

PhD students and academics with Ethiopian roots bring cultural fluency and a strong motivation to give back.

Research Topics Tied to Ethiopian Context

Students study problems relevant to Ethiopia — public health, urban development, education access, and more.

Access model: Admission is merit-based — students are evaluated on curiosity, commitment, and readiness to be stretched, not on prior research credentials or pedigree.

Key adaptation rationale: Ethiopian high schoolers are largely trained in rote/exam-focused learning. Studies show 55% of students find science classes uninteresting or boring, and inquiry-based learning is rarely practiced. The program's first 4 weeks aim to build the research mindset of our students.

Teferra, T., Amare, A., Oumer, J., W/hanna, T., Aklilu, D., & Berhanu, A. (2018). Ethiopian education development roadmap (2018–30): An integrated executive summary. Ministry of Education, Federal Democratic Republic of Ethiopia.

Program Philosophy

The core belief: Ethiopian students are not behind — they're under-scaffolded. The talent is there. What's missing is exposure to the research process, access to academic resources, and a mentor who believes the question they're asking matters. This program provides all three.

Research as a skill, not a credential

The paper is the outcome — but asking rigorous questions, evaluating evidence, and arguing clearly are what students actually keep for life.

Ethiopia as a research site, not a footnote

Students are encouraged to study problems relevant to Ethiopia: public health, urban development, education access, policy gaps. Home context is a research advantage.

Community over isolation

Students work in mentor pods rather than purely 1-on-1. Peer learning combats dropout, especially across time zones.

Accessibility without sacrificing rigor

We are merit-based — not hyper-exclusive. But the work expected is real. Students who complete this program will have done genuine academic research.

16-Week Curriculum

The program is divided into four phases. Each phase has a clear purpose, concrete deliverables, and dedicated mentor touchpoints. Weeks without a listed deliverable are drafting or practice weeks intentionally light on submission pressure.

WK 1–4
Phase I
Research Foundations

Build the mindset. Most students have never read an academic paper or formed a research question. This phase normalizes that discomfort and builds core literacy before any paper is assigned.

Week 1
What is Research? + Program Orientation

Types of research (qualitative, quantitative, review). Why research matters in an Ethiopian context. Intro to the cohort, mentors, and tools. Overview of the 16-week arc.

↳ 1-page "Why I Research" reflection
Week 2
Reading Academic Papers

How to read an abstract, introduction, and conclusion without drowning in jargon. Practice with 2 accessible papers in the student's track (STEM or Social Science). Identifying claims, evidence, and gaps.

Week 3
Forming a Research Question

The difference between a topic and a question. Moving from "I'm interested in malaria" to "What factors predict malaria incidence in highland Ethiopia?" FINER framework (Feasible, Interesting, Novel, Ethical, Relevant).

↳ 3 candidate research questions (draft)
Week 4
Information Literacy + Source Evaluation

Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, African Journals Online (AJOL). Distinguishing peer-reviewed, grey literature, and non-academic sources. Intro to Zotero for citation management. CRAAP test for source quality.

↳ Annotated bibliography of 5 sources on chosen topic
WK 5–8
Phase II
Proposal & Literature Review

Lock in the research question and build the intellectual foundation. Students understand their topic deeply enough to know what hasn't been answered yet — and why their paper matters.

Week 5
Research Proposal Workshop

Finalizing the research question with mentor. Writing a short research proposal: question, significance, approach, expected contribution. Mentor feedback session (group).

↳ 1-page Research Proposal (approved by mentor)
Week 6
Methodology Selection

STEM: Literature review / meta-analysis, secondary data analysis, computational modeling, survey design. Social Science: Qualitative (interviews, case study), quantitative (survey, regression with existing data), comparative policy analysis. Students choose the most feasible method given their access.

Week 7
Building the Literature Review

Synthesizing sources — not summarizing them. Identifying debates, consensus, and gaps. Thematic organization. First mentor 1-on-1 check-in on reading progress. Academic writing conventions: hedging language, citation norms, formal tone.

Week 8
Research Ethics + Practical Setup

Informed consent basics. Responsible use of data. For students doing surveys: designing ethical, unbiased instruments. Setting up data collection tools (Google Forms, publicly available datasets). Mid-program check-in and cohort peer share.

↳ Literature Review draft (800–1,200 words)
WK 9–13
Phase III
Research, Analysis & Drafting

The heaviest phase. Students execute their methodology, interpret their findings, and write a full draft. Mentor sessions shift from guidance to feedback. Writing coach enters here.

Week 9
Data Collection / Primary Research

Students gather data (run surveys, analyze datasets, conduct literature synthesis). Mentor available for troubleshooting. For social science students: interview guide review and ethical conduct of interviews.

Week 10
Analysis & Interpretation

STEM: Descriptive statistics, simple regression, thematic coding if qualitative. Social Science: Pattern identification, comparative analysis, discourse analysis. Translating raw findings into claims supported by evidence. Mentor 1-on-1 (2nd session).

↳ Findings summary (bullet format) — 1 page
Week 11
Writing the Argument: Introduction + Methods

Academic writing workshop with writing coach (Session 1). How to open a paper — hook, context, gap, contribution. Writing the methodology section clearly enough for replication. Structuring a logical argument from evidence.

Week 12
Writing Discussion & Conclusion

Interpreting findings beyond the data — "so what?" How to acknowledge limitations without undermining the paper. Connecting back to literature. Writing coach session 2: argumentation and clarity. Avoiding common ESL writing pitfalls in academic English.

Week 13
Complete First Draft

Students submit a complete first draft. Mentors provide written feedback within 5 days. Group peer review session — students read and comment on each other's work using a structured rubric (claims, evidence, clarity, structure).

↳ Complete first draft (2,500–4,000 words)
WK 14–16
Phase IV
Revision, Publication & Symposium

Polish and present. The goal is a paper the student is genuinely proud of — and a presentation they can give at a university interview or in front of peers.

Week 14
Structured Revision

Students revise based on mentor + peer feedback. Mentor 1-on-1 (3rd and final session): line-by-line discussion of feedback. Writing coach available for a final polish session on request. Abstract writing workshop.

↳ Revised second draft + 250-word abstract
Week 15
Publication Targeting + Final Submission

Students select a target journal or competition with mentor guidance (see publication pathways). Final proofreading and formatting. Submission to publication venue. EthioWare's Research Compendium as guaranteed publication home for all completers.

↳ Final paper submitted to EthioWare Compendium
Week 16
Virtual Research Symposium

Each student presents their paper in a 7-minute presentation + 5-minute Q&A. Open to parents, Ethiopian academic guests, and program alumni. Students receive a Program Certificate and Mentor Evaluation Letter. Celebration of completion.

↳ Symposium presentation + Certificate

Research Tracks & Topics

Two tracks let students pursue work aligned with their academic strengths, while keeping every project grounded in problems that matter to Ethiopia.

STEM & Natural Sciences Track
Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Computer Science

Designed around problems Ethiopia faces directly, where secondary data and accessible methods make rigorous work possible without a lab.

Public Health & Epidemiology Environmental Science Nutrition & Food Systems AI & Technology in Ethiopia Water & Sanitation Agricultural Science Climate & Weather Patterns Math Education Infectious Disease Modeling
Primary methods: Secondary data analysis (WHO, World Bank, Ethiopian CSA datasets), literature reviews, meta-analysis, simple quantitative surveys, computational modeling.
Social Sciences & Policy Track
Economics, Sociology, Education, Governance

Focused on Ethiopia's social fabric and policy landscape. These papers often have the most direct local impact — and are highly valued in international university applications.

Education Access & Equity Youth Unemployment Gender & Women's Rights Urban Planning (Addis Ababa) Informal Economy Digital Finance & Fintech Ethiopian Electoral Policy Refugee & Displacement Mental Health Stigma
Primary methods: Qualitative interviews, comparative policy analysis, case studies, thematic literature review, discourse analysis, basic survey design.

A note on scope: First-time high school researchers should aim for a focused, answerable question — not a thesis. A paper analyzing gender disparities in one region's secondary schools is better than "fixing all of Ethiopian education." Mentors should actively scope down ambition in Week 5.

Mentorship Model

We adopt a pod model rather than a pure 1-on-1 model. This makes the program sustainable from day one, reduces dropout (students are accountable to peers, not just mentors), and reflects how real research teams work.

Research Mentor

PhD student, postdoc, or faculty — ideally Ethiopian diaspora or scholars with Ethiopia expertise. Leads a pod of 3–4 students in the same track. Guides research questions, methodology, and interpretation.

3 group sessions + 3 individual 1-on-1s per student

Writing Coach

Shared resource across 2–3 pods. Specializes in academic English writing. Runs workshops in Weeks 11–12 and is available by appointment for a 30-min individual session. Priority given to ESL-challenged students.

2 group workshops + 1 optional 1-on-1

Program Coordinator

EthioWare staff. Manages the Telegram group, sends weekly reminders, tracks milestone submissions, resolves issues, and keeps mentors and students on schedule. Single point of contact for logistics.

Weekly touchpoint + on-demand support

Mentor sourcing strategy: Prioritize Ethiopian diaspora PhD students at AAU, US, and European universities — they bring cultural fluency and lived understanding of the Ethiopian academic gap. Supplement with international volunteers. Compensate mentors with a modest honorarium per cohort, or offer co-authorship credit where appropriate.

Tools & Platform

All tools chosen for free access, low bandwidth, and mobile compatibility — the three constraints that matter most in Ethiopia's connectivity landscape.

Telegram Primary communication hub. Channel for announcements, group chat per pod, bot for milestone reminders. Works on low data. Students already use it.
EthioWare LMS Hosts weekly PDF templates, writing tutorials, and milestone tracking for every cohort. Mentor inline comments and version history for paper drafting.
Google Meet / Zoom Video sessions with mentors. Sessions recorded and shared for students with connectivity issues who miss live calls.
Google Scholar + AJOL Free academic search. AJOL (African Journals Online) specifically hosts Ethiopian and African research — critical for local context papers.
Zotero Free, open-source citation manager. Students install it once; it handles all referencing automatically. Critical for APA/Chicago formatting.
Google Forms + Sheets For students running surveys. Data auto-populates into Sheets for basic analysis. No statistical software license needed for entry-level quantitative work.

Offline-first principle: Every week's core materials — readings, instructions, templates — are distributed as PDFs via the LMS and Telegram at the start of each week. Students can download and work offline. Live sessions are supplementary, not the only path to content.

Student Selection

The program is merit-based but not hyper-exclusive. We're not trying to find students who already know how to research — we're looking for curiosity, commitment, and the capacity to be stretched. Target grade level: Grade 10–12 (ages 15–18).

Aim for 15–25 students per cohort (3–5 mentor pods of 4–5). This is manageable for a pilot, and allows enough density for a real peer community.

Written Application

300-word motivation letter: "What problem do you want to research and why does it matter to you?" Evaluates intellectual curiosity and topic readiness, not polish.

Academic Record

Strong grades in relevant subjects (Biology, Chemistry, Physics for STEM; History, Economics, Civics for Social Science). No minimum GPA cutoff — holistic review.

Short Written Prompt

Students read a one-page summary of a published paper and answer: "What is the paper's main argument? What question is left unanswered?" Tests analytical reading.

15-min Interview

Conversational. Focus: Is the student genuinely curious? Can they talk about something they find puzzling in their field? Do they understand this is a 16-week commitment?

Diversity principle: Actively recruit from schools outside Addis Ababa — Dire Dawa, Mekelle, Hawassa, Bahir Dar. Regional diversity of research perspectives makes the cohort stronger, not weaker. Consider reserving 20–30% of seats for students from regional schools with strong applications.

Publication Pathways

Every student who completes the program will have their paper published. The platform depends on quality. Mentors help students target the right venue.

GUARANTEED
EthioWare Research Compendium

EthioWare's own curated online publication — every completer's paper is published here. This is the floor, not the ceiling. It gives all students a citable, public URL for their work. Built as a legitimate, indexed resource from day one.

ACCESSIBLE
Journal of Student Research (JSR) / Curieux Academic Journal

Peer-reviewed student journals that actively publish high school research. Free to submit, open access. Strong papers from Weeks 14–15 should target these. JSR covers STEM and social science; Curieux leans STEM.

ACCESSIBLE
African Journals Online (AJOL) Student Section / Ethiopian Academic Conferences

AJOL hosts Ethiopian and African academic content. Some journals accept student submissions with faculty co-author. Submitting to Ethiopian university-run conferences (AAU, Jimma, Hawassa) gives students a local publication credential.

STRETCH
Regeneron ISEF / Scholastica / MIT Think

International competitions for outstanding papers. In a 25-student cohort, 1–3 exceptional papers should realistically be nominated by mentors for these.
